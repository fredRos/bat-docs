9 @section sec-mcmc-motiv Motivation
10 
11 The reason that BAT exists is that nearly any Bayesian analysis
12 these days is too complicated to be handled analytically. To address
13 typical questions like
14 
15 * What is known about a single parameter taking into account
16  the uncertainty on all other parameters?
17 * How are parameters correlated?
18 
19 one needs to be able to compute and visualize 1D and 2D *marginal distributions*; cf. @ref sec-marginalization. These are defined as integrals over the posterior; for example in 2D
20 \f{align}{
21  \label{eq:mcmc-marginal}
22  P(\theta_1, \theta_2 \cond D) = \int \prod_{i \ne 1,2} \rmdx{\theta_i} P(\vecth \cond D).
23 \f}
24 Therefore Bayesian inference in practice requires good integration techniques.
25 
26 
27 For low-dimensional problems, deterministic integration methods are
28 usually the fastest and most robust. For marginalization, BAT defaults
29 to evaluating on a grid, that is Riemann integration, for 1D and 2D problems.
30 
31 @see BCIntegrate::SetMarginalizationMethod(BCIntegrate::kMargGrid)
32 
33 When the number of parameters grows, the only feasible algorithms to
34 perform the integration are Monte Carlo methods; i.e., methods based
35 on random numbers.

39 @section sec-mcmc-integration Monte Carlo integration
40 
41 We begin with the *fundamental Monte Carlo* principle. Suppose we have a
42 probability density \f$P(\vecth)\f$, often called the *target*
43 density, and an arbitrary function \f$f(\vecth)\f$ with finite
44 expectation value under \f$P\f$
45 \f{align}{
46  \label{eq:mc-expect}
47  E_P[ f ] = \int \rmdx{ \vecth} P(\vecth) f(\vecth) < \infty .
48 \f}
49 Then a
50 set of draws \f$\{ \vecth_i:i=1 \dots N \}\f$ from the density \f$P\f$, that is \f$\vecth_i \sim P\f$, is
51 enough to estimate the expectation value. Specifically, the integral
52 can be replaced by the estimator (distinguished by the symbol
53 \f$\widehat{\phantom{a}}\f$)
54 \f{align}{ \label{eq:mc-expect-discrete} \boxed{
55 \widehat{E_P[f]} \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(\vecth_i), \;
56 \vecth \sim P }
57 \f}
58 As \f$N \to \infty\f$, the estimate
59 converges almost surely at a rate \f$\propto 1/\sqrt{N}\f$
60 by the strong law of large numbers if \f$\int \rmdx{ \vecth}
61 P(\vecth) f^2(\vecth) < \infty\f$ @cite Casella:2004 .
62 
63 @anchor mcmc-histogram
64 @image html histogram.png "Histogram approximation to the 1D marginal."
65 @image latex histogram.pdf "Histogram approximation to the 1D marginal." width=0.3\textwidth
66 
67 How does this @latexonly Eq.~\ref{eq:mc-expect-discrete}@endlatexonly
68 relate to Bayesian inference? Upon applying Bayes’ theorem to
69 real-life problems, one usually has to marginalize over several
70 parameters, and this can usually not be done analytically, hence one
71 has to resort to numerical techniques. In low dimensions, say \f$d \le
72 2\f$, quadrature and other grid-based methods are fast and accurate,
73 but as \f$d\f$ increases, these methods generically suffer from the
74 *curse of dimensionality*. The number of function evaluations grows
75 exponentially as \f$\order{m^d}\f$, where \f$m\f$ is the number of
76 grid points in one dimension. Though less accurate in few dimensions,
77 Monte Carlo — i.e., random-number based — methods are the first choice
78 in \f$d \gtrsim 3\f$ because the computational complexity is (at least
79 in principle) independent of \f$d\f$. Which function \f$f\f$ is of
80 interest to us? For example when integrating over all but the first
81 dimension of \f$\vecth\f$, the marginal posterior probability
82 that \f$\theta_1\f$ is in \f$[a,b]\f$ can be estimated as
83 
84 \f[
85 \label{eq:disc-marg} P(a \le \theta_1 \le b|D) \approx \frac{1}{N}
86 \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbf{1}_{\theta_1 \in [a,b]} (\vecth_i) \, \f]
87 with the *indicator function*
88 \f[ \label{eq:indicator-fct}
89 \mathbf{1}_{\theta_1 \in [a,b]} (\vecth) = \begin{cases} 1, \theta_1
90 \in [a,b] \\ 0, {\rm else} \end{cases}
91 \f]
92 
93 This follows immediately from the Monte Carlo principle with
94 \f$f(\vecth) = \mathbf{1}_{\theta_1 \in [a,b]}(\vecth)\f$. The major
95 simplification arises as we perform the integral over \f$d-1\f$
96 dimensions simply by ignoring these dimensions in the indicator
97 function. If the parameter range of \f$\theta_1\f$ is partitioned into
98 bins, then the above holds in every bin, and defines the histogram
99 approximation to \f$P(\theta_1|D)\f$. In exact analogy, the 2D
100 histogram approximation is computed from the samples for 2D bins in
101 the indicator function. For understanding and presenting the results
102 of Bayesian parameter inference, the set of 1D and 2D marginal
103 distributions is the primary goal. Given samples from the full
104 posterior, we have immediate access to *all* marginal distributions at
105 once; i.e., there is no need for separate integration to obtain for
106 example \f$P(\theta_1|D)\f$ and \f$P(\theta_2|D)\f$. This is a major
107 benefit of the Monte Carlo method in conducting Bayesian inference.

109 @section sec-mcmc-foundations Foundations
110 
111 The key ingredient in BAT is an implementation of the Metropolis
112 algorithm to create a Markov chain; i.e. a sequence of (correlated)
113 samples from the posterior. We use the shorthand MCMC for Markov chain
114 Monte Carlo.
115 
116 Efficient MCMC algorithms are the topic of past and current
117 research. This section is a concise overview of the general idea and
118 the algorithms available in BAT. For a broader overview, we refer the
119 reader to the abundant literature; e.g., @todo Robert+Casella, MCMC
120  handbook.
121 
122 In BAT, there are several variants of the random-walk Metropolis Hastings algorithm available. The basic idea is captured in the @ref random-walk-2D "2D example plot". Given an initial point \f$x_0\f$, the Metropolis algorithm produces a sample in each iteration \f$t=1 \dots\f$ as follows:
123 
124 * Propose a new point \f$y\f$
125 * Generate a number u from the uniform distribution on [0,1]
126 * Set \f$x_{t} = y\f$ if \f$ u < \frac{P(y \cond D)}{P(x_{t-1} \cond D)}\f$
127 * Else stay, \f$x_t = x_{t-1}\f$
128 
129 @anchor random-walk-2D
130 @image html random-walk.png "2D random walk with the Metropolis algorithm."
131 @image latex random-walk.pdf "2D random walk with the Metropolis algorithm." width=0.5\textwidth
132 
133 In the example plot, the chain begins in the lower left
134 corner. Rejected moves are indicated by the dashed arrow, accepted
135 moves are indicated by the solid arrow. The circled number is the
136 number of iterations the chain stays at a given point \f$\vecth =
137 (\theta_1, \theta_2)\f$.
138 
139 In each iteration \f$i\f$, one updates the estimate of the 1D marginal
140 distribution \f$P(\theta_1 | D)\f$ by adding the first coordinate of
141 \f$\vecth_i\f$ to a histogram. Repeat this for all other coordinates
142 to update the other \f$d-1\f$ 1D marginals.

150 @section sec-mcmc-impl Implementation in BAT
151 
152 Implementing the Metropolis algorithm, one has to decide on how to
153 propose a new point based on the current point, that is one needs the
154 *proposal distribution* \f$q(y \cond x, \xi)\f$ with adjustable parameters
155 \f$\xi\f$. The main difference between MCMC algorithms is typically given
156 by different choices of \f$q\f$. The Metropolis algorithm doesn't specify
157 which \f$q\f$ to choose, so we can and have to select a function \f$q\f$ and
158 tune \f$\xi\f$ according to our needs.
159 
160 In BAT, the proposal is *symmetric* around the current point
161 \f{align}{
162  q(y \cond x, \xi) = q(x \cond y, \xi).
163 \f}
164 
165 The Markov property implies that the proposal may only depend on the
166 current point \f$x\f$ and not on previous points. If the value of
167 \f$\xi\f$ is set based on a past sequence of iterations of the chain,
168 we need two stages of sampling in BAT, the *prerun* and the *main
169 run*. In the prerun, the chain is run and periodically \f$\xi\f$ is
170 updated based on the past iterations. In contrast, \f$\xi\f$ is kept
171 fixed in the main run to have a proper Markov chain.
172 
173 ## Prerun {#sec-mcmc-prerun}
174 
175 During the prerun, the proposal is updated. BAT considers three criteria to decide when to end the prerun:
176 
177 ### Efficiency {#sec-mcmc-eff}
178 
179 The *efficiency*, or acceptance rate, is the ratio of the accepted over the total number proposal moves. A small efficiency means the chain rarely moves but may then make a large move. A large efficiency means the chain explores well locally but may take a long time to explore the entire region of high probability. Optimality results exists only for very special cases: Roberts and Rosenthal showed that for a Gaussian target with \f$d\f$ independent components and a Gaussian proposal, the optimal target efficiency is 23.4 % for \f$d \geq 5\f$ is but should be larger for small \f$d\f$; e.g., 44 % is best in one dimension @cite rosenthal2011optimal . Based on our experience, we use a default range for the efficiency as \f$[0.15, 0.35]\f$.
180 
181 @see BCEngineMCMC::SetMinimumEfficiency, BCEngineMCMC::SetMaximumEfficiency
182 
183 ### R value {#sec-mcmc-Rvalue}
184 
185 The *R value* @cite Gelman:1992 by Gelman and Rubin quantifies the
186 estimated scale reduction of the uncertainty of an expectation value
187 estimated with the samples if the chain were run infinitely
188 long. Informally, it compares the mean and variance of the expectation
189 value for a single chain with the corresponding results of multiple
190 chains. If the chains mix despite different initial values, then we
191 assume that they are independent of the initial value, the burn-in is
192 over, and the samples produce reliable estimates of quantities of
193 interest. For a single chain, the \f$R\f$ value cannot be computed.
194 
195 In BAT, we monitor the expectation value of each parameter and declare
196 convergence if all R values are below a threshold. Note that the R
197 values are estimated from batches of samples, and they usually
198 decrease with more iterations but they may also increase, which
199 usually is a clear indication that the chains do not mix, perhaps due
200 to multiple modes that trap the chains.
201 
202 @see BCEngineMCMC::SetRValueParametersCriterion Set that maximum R value for all parameters. **Default: 1.1**
203 
204 @see BCEngineMCMC::SetCorrectRValueForSamplingVariability The strict definition of \f$R\f$ corrects the sampling variability due finite batch size. **Default: false**
205 
206 @see BCEngineMCMC::GetRValueParameters \f$R\f$ values are computed during the prerun and they can be retrieved but not set.
207 
208 ### Prerun length {#sec-mcmc-prerun-length}
209 
210 Defining convergence automatically based on the efficiency or the \f$R\f$ value is convenient may be too conservative if the user knows a good initial value, a good proposal, etc. For more control the minimum and maximum length of the prerun can be set, too.
211 
212 @see BCEngineMCMC::SetNIterationsPreRunMin, BCEngineMCMC::SetNIterationsPreRunMax
213 
214 @see BCEngineMCMC::SetNIterationsPreRunCheck sets the number of iterations between checks
215 
216 If desired, the statistics can be cleared to remove the effect of a bad initial point with BCEngineMCMC::SetPreRunCheckClear after some set of iterations

222 ## Proposal functions {#sec-mcmc-proposal}
223 
224 BAT offers two kinds of proposal function termed *factorized* and *multivariate*.
225 
226 ### Factorized proposal {#sec-factorized}
227 
228 @since Factorized was the default and only choice prior to v1.0 and continues to be available using BCEngineMCMC::SetProposeMultivariate(true)
229 
230 The factorized proposal in \f$d\f$ dimensions is a product of 1D proposals.
231 
232 We sequentially vary one parameter at a time and complete
233 one iteration of the chain once a new point has been proposed in *every*
234 direction. This means the chain attempts to perform a sequence of
235 axis-aligned moves in one iteration.
236 
237 @todo Easiest to understand would be pseudocode
238 
239 Each 1D proposal is a Cauchy or Breit-Wigner function centered on the
240 current point. The scale parameter is adapted in the prerun to achieve
241 an acceptance rate in a given range that can be adjusted by the
242 user. Note that there is a separate scale parameter in every dimension.
243 
244 This means the posterior is called \f$d\f$ times in every iteration. Since
245 the acceptance rate
246 
247 @todo to define before
248 
249 is typically different from
250 zero or one, the factorized proposal typically generates a new point
251 in every iteration that differs from the previous point in some but
252 not all dimensions.
253 
254 ### Multivariate proposal {#sec-multivariate}
255 
256 @since 1.0
257 
258 Changing all \f$d\f$ parameters at once within one iteration is an
259 all-or-nothing approach. If the proposed move is accepted, all
260 parameters have changed for the price of a single evaluation of the
261 posterior. If the move is rejected, the new point is identical to the
262 old point and the chain does not explore the parameter space.
263 
264 We implement the adaptive algorithm @cite Haario:2001 by Haario et al. In
265 brief, the proposal is a multivariate Gaussian or Student's t
266 distribution whose covariance is learned from the covariance of
267 samples in the prerun. An overall scale factor is tuned to force the
268 acceptance rate into a certain range.

283 ## Comparison {#sec-mcmc-proposal-comparison}
284 
285 Comparing the factorized proposal to the multivariate proposal, we
286 generally recommend the multivariate for most purposes.
287 
288 Use the factorized proposal if you can speed up the computation of the
289 posterior if you know that some parameters did not change. This can be
290 useful if the computation is expensive if some but not all
291 parameters change.
